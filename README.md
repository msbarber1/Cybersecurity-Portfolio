## About Me

I'm a working cybersecurity practitioner with a background that spans military
network defense, vulnerability management, incident response, and enterprise
security architecture. After more than two decades in the U.S. Air Force and
Air Reserve components — where I supported network defense, vulnerability
remediation, and security engineering across mission-critical environments —
I transitioned into healthcare and now serve as an Enterprise Security
Architect at MaineHealth, where I focus on cloud security, HIPAA-regulated
data protection, and the responsible adoption of AI capabilities in clinical
and administrative workflows.

My interest in cybersecurity started early in my military career, when I saw
firsthand how much the integrity of a network, the trustworthiness of a
system, and the discipline of a defender could matter to a mission. That
operational mindset has shaped how I approach the field ever since: security
has to be effective *and* realistic, and the best defenders are the ones who
keep learning.

I'm currently pursuing a Bachelor of Applied Cybersecurity (BACS) at the
SANS Technology Institute, hold the GCIH, GSEC, and GFACT certifications,
serve on the GIAC Advisory Board, and continue to develop hands-on skills
through coursework, lab work, and competitions like NCL.

## Reflection

Competing in NCL was one of the most worthwhile experiences I've had as a
student. The challenges are designed to mirror real-world scenarios across
the full breadth of the cybersecurity discipline, and they pushed me well
beyond what any single course or job role would on its own.

A few things stood out to me:

**Breadth matters.** Working through nine distinct skill categories in a
single weekend forces you to context-switch the way real defenders do —
from cryptanalysis one minute to log review the next, then to a web
exploitation problem, then to packet analysis. Comfort across that breadth
is something I'll keep building.

**Depth comes from time under pressure.** Several of the harder challenges
required pulling together skills I'd practiced in isolation and applying
them in combination. Solving them taught me as much about my own thinking
process — where I got stuck, what I rushed past, what I should have read
more carefully — as it did about any particular tool or technique.

**Teamwork multiplies what's possible.** The Team Game taught me how much
faster a strong team can move when communication is good and people lean
into their strengths. Watching teammates approach a problem in a way I
hadn't considered was consistently one of the best parts of the experience.

**There is always more to learn.** Even where I placed well, the categories
where I lost the most points are the ones I'm most excited to come back to.
That's the point — the competition is a measuring stick, not a finish line.

In keeping with NCL's policy, I'm not publishing challenge-specific
write-ups here. The scouting report links above provide a validated record
of the categories, challenges attempted, and performance.
